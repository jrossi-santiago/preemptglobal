Day 1 — Doc

The RFI Response-Time Framework kills the silent RFI graveyard — the pile of unanswered requests nobody's tracking until the schedule blows up.

Most teams log RFIs the day they're sent and forget them until someone asks where the schedule went. Three checkpoints fix that:

Day 3: escalate anything without a response past 3 business days. Most contracts specify 5-10 days, but waiting for the deadline means you've already lost the float.

Day 7: flag it to the PM directly, not just the log. A red cell in a spreadsheet nobody opens doesn't escalate anything.

Day 14: if it's still open, it's a delay claim in the making. Document the cost impact now, not after the fact when nobody remembers why the wall got framed wrong.

Run this on a $30M project and the pattern shows up fast: it's rarely the RFI that gets answered slow that causes damage. It's the one that gets answered wrong because nobody flagged it as urgent until the trade was already standing around waiting.

Pull your open RFI log this week and check it against these three checkpoints. If more than a handful are past day 7, you already know where next month's delay claim is coming from.

Day 2 — Story

Your site visit reports from six months ago are the only proof you have when a claim shows up.

Case in point: a punch list dispute on a $40M mixed-use project. The GC claimed a corridor was substantially complete on a given date; the owner disagreed, said punch items were still open. Six months later, nobody remembered clearly, and real money was riding on the exact date.

The site visit report from that week said "Corridor 3B: taping and finish coat in progress, no fixtures installed." One sentence. It settled the dispute in an afternoon instead of a deposition.

Most site visit reports are written for the file, not for the fight. They log presence, not condition. "Attended site visit, no issues to report" doesn't help anyone six months later when someone's arguing about substantial completion.

The fix isn't longer reports. It's specific ones: what trade was working, what was and wasn't installed, dated photos tied to the sheet number they relate to. Five extra minutes per report is the difference between a one-page answer and a six-figure argument nobody can settle.

Go back and read your last three site visit reports. If you can't tell what was actually built from reading them, that's the gap to close before your next one.

Day 3 — Doc

The Set-of-Record Protocol kills the addenda dispute — the one where two trades build off different versions of the same sheet and nobody can agree whose fault it is.

It's three rules, not a software platform:

One version, one owner. A single named person, not "the office," owns the current set-of-record, and every addendum gets logged against it within 24 hours of issue.

Superseded means stamped, not deleted. Old sheets don't disappear from the job trailer. They get stamped VOID and stay in a binder nobody's supposed to build from but everyone can check.

Every RFI response that changes a drawing gets a bulletin number, not a verbal "yeah, use the new one." If it's not in writing with a number attached, it didn't happen when the change order fight starts.

Most document control failures aren't dramatic. Nobody loses a whole drawing set. It's one super running an addendum behind because he was in the field all week, and one trade framing a wall to a dimension that changed eleven days ago.

Check your addenda log against what's actually posted in the trailer this week. The gap between the two is usually bigger than you think, and it's cheaper to close now than after the wall's closed up.

Day 4 — Story

Your switchgear order placed today lands in 44 weeks. Your schedule says 30.

That gap showed up on a data center bid set: the electrical spec called for gear whose lead time, at bid, was already running 10-12 months longer than the construction schedule accounted for. Nobody had cross-checked the two.

It's an easy miss. The spec section and the schedule live in different documents, built by different people, on different timelines. The spec writer isn't watching supplier lead times week to week. The scheduler isn't reading switchgear cut sheets.

By the time someone catches it, usually when the sub tries to place the order and gets a delivery date past substantial completion, the options are all bad: expedite fees running 15-20% over list price, a redesign around interim gear, or a schedule slip that cascades through every trade behind electrical.

Caught at bid review, it's a phone call to the supplier to confirm current lead time and a schedule adjustment. Caught six months in, it's a change order and a hard conversation with the owner about why the building isn't ready on the date everyone signed up for.

Cross-check your long-lead items against current supplier lead times this week, not the lead times from when the spec was written. They've moved.

Day 5 — Midas

$41,000 came off a change order in one afternoon, no negotiation required.

A GC submitted a change order for added structural steel tied to a design revision: $41K in material and labor. The backup showed the steel tonnage. It didn't show labor hours broken out by task, and the material pricing referenced a quote dated four months before the order was actually placed. Steel pricing had dropped 12% in that window.

The owner's rep didn't dispute that the change was legitimate. The design revision was real, the steel was needed. What got challenged was the pricing: an outdated quote, no labor breakdown to check against, markup stacked on top of already-marked-up sub pricing.

Revised backup came back within a week. Same scope, $41K lower, because the actual quote at time of order and an itemized labor breakdown were now on the table.

Most change order disputes aren't about whether the work happened. They're about whether the backup supports the number. GCs know most owner's reps don't have time to check a quote date against a steel price index.

Before you approve the next change order over $25K, check the quote date against current material pricing. It's a five-minute check that pays for itself more often than not.

Day 6 — Conviction

Clash detection cannot catch the thing nobody modeled. That's not a software limitation. It's a logic limitation, and no update fixes it.

A clash run finds a duct hitting a beam because both are in the model and the software checks geometry against geometry. It cannot flag a generator that was never modeled at all, because there's nothing to check against nothing. The model doesn't know a generator is missing. It just quietly has no generator.

I've seen teams treat a clean clash report as a clean set. It isn't. It's a set with no geometric conflicts between the things someone remembered to model. Scope gaps, missing systems, spec-to-drawing mismatches: none of that shows up as a clash, because none of it is a collision. It's an absence, and absences don't clash with anything.

The industry keeps buying more clash detection software to solve a problem clash detection was never built to catch. That's not a knock on the tool. It's a knock on treating a clean run as proof of a complete set.

If your QC process ends the moment the clash report comes back clean, you've checked the geometry and skipped the completeness. Those are two different questions, and only one of them has software for it.

Day 7 — Vulnerability

I missed a warranty exclusion once, early on, and it cost a client real money.

Reviewing a roofing spec on one of my first jobs on the owner's side, I checked the fire rating, the wind uplift requirements, the flashing details, all the things I'd been trained to check. I didn't check whether the installer certification the manufacturer required matched who was actually bidding the work.

Eighteen months after substantial completion, a section of membrane failed. The owner filed a warranty claim. The manufacturer denied it: the installing contractor wasn't on their certified applicator list, so the 20-year warranty was void from day one. Nobody had caught it, including me, because it wasn't on the checklist I was running. It was buried in a manufacturer's spec addendum, not the base spec.

That miss cost the owner a $60,000 reroof out of pocket, on a warranty they thought they had.

I don't run a review now without checking installer certification against manufacturer requirements on anything with a material warranty attached. It's a five-minute check today. It wasn't on my radar at all back then.

What's not on your checklist that should be? Usually you don't find out until it costs somebody six figures.
