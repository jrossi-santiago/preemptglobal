01

The gap

Three people read your drawings. None of them work for you.

On every large project, the same three parties open the same documents — and read them for three completely different reasons. Everyone is doing their job exactly as their incentives ask them to. The problem is that none of those incentives are yours.

A
The architect

Checks for design intent — then stops.

QCs the set against their own drawings. Once it matches what they intended to draw, their review is done.

C
The contractor

Reads it like a chess board.

Hunts for the gaps and ambiguities they can price later as change orders. A conflict found now is revenue found later.

O
The owner — you

Pays for every miss. Reviewed by no one.

The only party in the room with no one reading the set purely on their behalf — and the only one who writes the check.

That gap is structural, not accidental. It is also exactly where the money leaks.

02

What the gap costs

The same conflict costs nothing on paper — and a fortune in the field.

Design$0
Permit$1K–$5K
Bid$5K–$50K
Field$20K–$200K+

A coordination conflict doesn't get more complicated as a project moves forward. It gets more expensive. The identical clash, caught at four different moments, carries four very different price tags.

The only variable that moves a conflict up that ladder is whether someone catches it before the set issues. Once a set goes to bid, the cheap fix is gone for good — and every uncaught conflict has already started climbing.

Before you decide

A few things owners ask first.

How does confidentiality work?

An NDA is signed before any drawings move. Nothing is sent until it's in place — and the set is ours to review, not to share, store loosely, or keep past the engagement. E&O coverage is carried on every engagement, and your documents are deleted on completion.

Doesn't our GC's or architect's review already cover this?

No — and that's the whole point. The architect QCs the set against their own design intent. The contractor reads it for what they can price later. Both reviews are real; neither is run for you. This one is — paid by the owner, working for no one else. The case above is a set that had already been through a top-5 GC's coordination process.

Doesn't BIM clash detection catch this already?

Clash detection catches geometry — two elements overlapping inside the coordinated model. It doesn't catch the model contradicting the written spec, a general note that prohibits what another sheet requires, or details that don't overlap in the model but still stop work in the field. We read the issued set itself, which is where most of these conflicts actually live.

What kinds of projects do you review?

Large commercial and industrial builds — office, manufacturing, warehouse and distribution, healthcare, higher-ed, data centers, and other complex, multi-discipline sets. If it's $30M+ in construction value with multiple trades sharing tight coordination, it's the kind of set this review is built for.

What do we actually get back?

A written findings report: every conflict located by sheet and detail, severity-tiered, with an estimated field cost on each — plus a 30-minute debrief to walk it. Specific findings like Exhibit A above, never a vague "area of concern."
