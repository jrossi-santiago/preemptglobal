What to resolve before award

Ranked, not just listed.

Top 3 priorities

Exactly as they'd appear in your debrief call.

  1. Issue the missing G2.4.X alternate drawings. Referenced 12+ times across every discipline; the set cannot be fully priced without them.
  2. Design and add backup power (UPS/generator) and server-room fire suppression. Both are complete omissions — S103 is permit-blocked without a suppression system.
  3. Lock down the CRAC package: correct the condenser model number, then reconcile the feeder and breaker sizing on panel ISACHA against the selected unit's actual nameplate MCA/MOCP. With precision-cooling lead times running 20–40 weeks, this is the finding with the least room to be late.

Why this file exists

A description of what we do isn't evidence that we do it well.

So instead of telling you about the process, we're giving you the output — unedited except for the names, at the same level of specificity a paying client gets: sheet reference, mechanism, dollar range, schedule impact, one line each.

Twenty-six findings. Four critical. All twenty-six turned up on a set that had already been through a general contractor's own internal coordination review — which is the case for why a dedicated, owner-side pass is worth running alongside it, not instead of it. One independent, cross-discipline pass: drawings vs. drawings, drawings vs. specs, trade vs. trade.

Software flags maybes. This file puts a dollar figure — and our own fee — on every one of them.

Note on cost basis. Five line items in the original review (backup power, fire suppression, storefront, wet-pipe reroute, dimension corrections) reflect figures from that review, used as-is. The remaining line items are order-of-magnitude estimates for comparable RFI, redesign, and engineering scope, informed by current (Q1–Q2 2026) nonresidential construction cost trends. All 2021 dollar figures are escalated by a 1.26x factor to mid-2026 dollars. That factor is deliberately conservative: nonresidential buildings final-cost inflation ran roughly 8% in 2021 and 12% in 2022 — the sharpest two-year run since 1980–81 — and cumulative final-cost indices from mid-2021 to mid-2026 track closer to 1.35–1.40x (per Construction Analytics' composite index; the Mortenson Quarterly Cost Index alone rose 6.8% in the trailing 12 months as of Q1 2026). We carry the lower factor so every figure on this page understates, rather than overstates, current-dollar exposure. Actual costs should be verified against qualified contractor and current supplier quotes — this is a coordination-risk estimate, not a bid.
